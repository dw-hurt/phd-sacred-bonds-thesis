## Chapter 2: Evolutionary Psychology and Sexual Selection

### Quote 1: Core Definition of Hypergamy
> "We use the term hypergamy to describe a phenomenon whereby there is a tendency for husbands to be of higher rank within the male earnings capacity distribution than their wives are within the female distribution."

**Page:** 1 (Abstract)  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 2.1:** Define hypergamy in evolutionary context
- Core operational definition for empirical analysis
- Distinguishes from educational hypergamy (different distributions)
- Links to Bateman's principle (reproductive variance)
- Sets up framework for understanding sexual selection in modern humans

---

### Quote 2: Empirical Evidence for Hypergamy
> "Based on complete multigenerational data for all offspring born from 1952 through 1975, we show that there is a steeper positive relationship between own earnings-potential rank and the probability of finding a partner for men than for women and that there are more unmatched men than women, particularly at the bottom of the rank distribution. We also show that men with higher rank tend to mate multiple times, and that the man's rank tends to exceed the woman's rank within couples. Together, these findings present strong evidence in support of hypergamy."

**Page:** 3-4  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 2.2:** Sexual Strategies Theory empirical validation
- **Section 2.3:** Bateman's principle—high variance in male reproductive success
- Direct evidence: high-status men mate multiple times, low-status men don't mate
- Parallels animal mating systems (polygyny, sexual selection)
- Strongest empirical support for evolutionary psychology predictions

---

### Quote 3: Male Mating Success Gradient
> "For men, a man at the top of the earnings distribution has more than a 90 percent chance of having found a partner, the chance of a man at the bottom is less than 40 percent. For women, similar chances across the earnings distribution, except at the very bottom."

**Page:** 11  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 2.3:** Parental investment theory—female choice drives sexual selection
- Massive 50+ percentage point gap between high/low status men
- Women's partnership probability nearly flat (~80-85% across distribution)
- Visualizes reproductive variance: male success highly variable, female success universal
- Supports "male competition, female choice" model

---

### Quote 4: Fertility Mechanism (Biological Explanation)
> "The first [mechanism] operates through fertility differences, and relates to the biological fact that men are reproductive for a longer period than women. As previously highlighted by, e.g., Siow (1998) and Polachek et al. (2015), this implies that fertile women are relatively scarce and can be choosy with respect to a partner's attributes such as education or earnings potential."

**Page:** 5  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 2.4:** Biological constraints on mating systems
- Male reproductive window: ~15-70+ (55 years)
- Female reproductive window: ~15-45 (30 years)
- Scarcity creates bargaining power → female selectivity
- Links to parental investment theory (Trivers, 1972)

---

### Quote 5: Gendered Mate Preferences (Evolutionary Psychology)
> "This preference mechanism fits well with a literature indicating that men give more weight to physical attractiveness and beauty than do women, and that women give more weight to IQ and earnings potential (Davis, 1941; Elder, 1969; Buss, 1989; Buss and Schmidt, 2019; Cashdan, 1996; Fisman et al., 2006; Hitsch et al., 2010; Eastwick et al., 2014, Buunk et al., 2002)."

**Page:** 5-6  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 2.5:** Cross-cultural universality of mate preferences
- Cites Buss (1989)—37 cultures study (foundational evolutionary psychology)
- Men prioritize fertility cues (youth, beauty)
- Women prioritize provisioning ability (resources, status)
- Pattern predicted by parental investment theory

---

### Quote 6: Survey Experiment Results (Empirical Preference Data)
> "There is a significant positive treatment effect for both men and women. For men, the estimated treatment effect on a normalized scale is 0.173 (p-value = 0.026) whereas for women, it is 0.380 (p-value < 0.000). The coefficient for a difference-in-difference estimator is thus equal to 0.206 (p-value = 0.042). Hence, this experiment confirms that in a representative sample of Norwegians, females give more weight to the earnings of a prospective partner than males do."

**Page:** 19-20  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 2.5:** Direct experimental evidence for preference asymmetry
- Women weight partner earnings **2.2x more** than men (0.380 vs 0.173)
- Statistically significant difference (p=0.042)
- Representative national sample (not convenience sample)
- Confirms preferences drive behavioral patterns observed in register data

---

### Quote 7: Historical Genetic Evidence
> "The fertility mechanism is also consistent with historical patterns as recent research in genetics shows that women to a larger extent than men have passed on their DNA, which again is consistent with a larger fraction of women than men mating in human history (Wilder et al., 2004; Keinan and Clark, 2012; Lippold et al., 2014; Karmin et al., 2015)."

**Page:** 37 (Online Appendix)  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 2.6:** Deep evolutionary time perspective
- Karmin et al. (2015): 17 women reproduced for every 1 man during bottleneck
- Historical polygyny much more extreme than modern hypergamy
- Hypergamy is **attenuated** version of ancestral mating system
- Provides evolutionary context for contemporary patterns

## Chapter 3: Sociological Mismatch and Contemporary Crisis

### Quote 8: Gender Equality Paradox
> "The finding of hypergamy based on a characteristic that by construction has exactly the same distribution for men and women suggests that full gender-equality in earnings potentials may not be a sufficient condition for ensuring gender-equality in actual earnings. Hypergamy implies that his earnings potential will tend to exceed hers even in such cases, and this is likely to have implications for the division of market and household work within the household."

**Page:** 4  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 3.2:** Sociological mismatch framework—gender equality doesn't eliminate evolved patterns
- Even with **identical distributions**, hypergamy produces asymmetric outcomes
- Core paradox: more equality → persistent hypergamy (not convergence)
- Challenges assumptions of social constructionism
- Supports WEIRD-STRANGELY critique (Fayyaz, 2019)

---

### Quote 9: Household Specialization Mechanism
> "According to standard human capital theory, a higher earnings potential for the male partner implies an incentive for household specialization whereby his labor market career is prioritized (Becker, 1991). Through learning-by-doing, the initial difference in human capital may be enlarged over time, yielding even stronger incentives for household specialization (Mincer, 1974; Becker, 1993; Polachek et al., 2015; Angelov et al., 2016; Schaller, 2016; Gihleb and Lifshitz, 2016). Hence, hypergamy can give rise to a marital gender earnings gap that widens over the lifecycle, and that continues to prevail (although at a lower level) even if discrimination and other obstacles women face in the labor market cease to exist."

**Page:** 4-5  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 3.3:** Household specialization as emergent property of hypergamous sorting
- Initial 4.5 percentile gap (hypergamy) → 7-8 percentile gap (specialization) over lifecycle
- Learning-by-doing amplifies initial differences
- Gender wage gap persists **without discrimination** (structural, not discriminatory)
- Implications for feminist policy: equal pay laws insufficient

---

### Quote 10: Persistence Despite Egalitarianism
> "Although the United Nations over the last 15 years has repeatedly declared that Norway is the most gender-equal society in the world, substantial gender differences in pay and employment patterns remain. In this paper, we have offered one explanation as to why gendered employment and earnings patterns may persist even with full gender equality in labor market opportunities; i.e., even in a society where the distributions of earnings-potential are identical and where there is no gender discrimination. The channel is the matching of men and women into households and the subsequent division of market and household work."

**Page:** 22  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 3.4:** Contemporary relevance—mating market structure drives gender gaps
- Norway = most egalitarian society possible, yet hypergamy persists
- Conclusion: **Biology constrains sociology** (not determinism, but constraint)
- Mating market is **upstream** of labor market outcomes
- Need to address household formation, not just workplace policies

---

### Quote 11: Unmatched Men Crisis
> "Households are systematically formed such that the man on average has the highest rank within the gender-specific distribution of earnings potential, and men with very poor earnings prospects have a high probability of staying unmatched."

**Page:** 23  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 3.5:** "Incel" phenomenon and male unmatching
- Structural exclusion of low-status men from mating market
- Parallels dating app data (bottom 50% of men receive <5% of female attention)
- Social consequences: frustrated male mate aspiration → societal instability
- NOT individual failure—structural outcome of hypergamous sorting

---

### Quote 12: Partnership Probability by Status
> "Probability of having found a partner is 7–8 percentage points higher for a man born into the richest parental earnings vigintile than for a man born into the poorest parental earnings vigintile. For women, there is hardly a visible social gradient."

**Page:** 12  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 3.5:** Quantifies status-based exclusion
- Male partnership: **steep gradient** (7-8 percentage points across distribution)
- Female partnership: **flat** (nearly universal regardless of status)
- Visualizes Bateman's principle in human populations
- Supports evolutionary mismatch hypothesis (Chapter 9)

## Chapter 4: Psychological/Individual Dimension (Jungian)

### Quote 13: Gendered Attribute Weighting
> "Men and women care about features other than earnings when choosing a partner, such as physical attractiveness and the ability of caring/parenthood, and may weight these attributes differently. Our driving assumption is that females give more weight to income potential than men when choosing between partners."

**Page:** 5  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 4.3:** Anima/animus projection in mate choice
- Women weight income potential → seeking external animus (competence, agency, power)
- Men weight attractiveness → seeking external anima (beauty, fertility, nurturance)
- Preferences = **projected unconscious** contents
- Shadow work: integrating these qualities internally to transcend projection-based choice

---

### Quote 14: Marital Instability When Women Earn More
> "It is also consistent with the findings that marital stability and satisfaction tend to be lower when women earn more than their partners (Bertrand et al., 2015) and that divorce rates increase when women become promoted (Folke and Rickne, 2018)."

**Page:** 6  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 4.4:** Ego threat and shadow activation
- Male ego threatened when woman out-earns him → relationship instability
- Shadow material: male inferiority complex, inadequacy, castration anxiety
- Female promotion → male withdrawal or conflict
- Individuation path: men integrate shadow to tolerate female success

## Chapter 5: Archetypal/Collective Unconscious

### Quote 15: Hypergamy as Archetypal Pattern
> "There is also a literature pointing toward gender asymmetries in mating patterns, such that women are, on average, likely to mate with men of higher economic and social status than themselves. This phenomenon is sometimes referred to as hypergamy, and it may imply that husbands tend to have higher human capital than wives within couples, even in situations where the unconditional distributions of human capital for men and women are exactly the same."

**Page:** 1-2  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 5.2:** Hypergamy as archetypal (not merely cultural)
- Cross-cultural universality suggests **collective unconscious** pattern
- Women seeking King archetype (sovereignty, resources, protection)
- Men embodying Warrior archetype (competition, status achievement)
- Hieros Gamos tension: archetypal ideal (symmetry) vs. biological reality (asymmetry)

## Chapter 7: Synchronicity and Meaningful Coincidence

### Quote 17: Assortative Mating vs. Hypergamy
> "The social sciences indicate that humans generally tend to find partners who are similar economically and socially, i.e., we display homogamy/assortative mating (Fernandez et al., 2005; Schwartz and Mare, 2005; Browning et al., 2014; Greenwood et al., 2014; Bratsberg et al., 2018; Eika et al., 2019). However, there is also a literature pointing toward gender asymmetries in mating patterns, such that women are, on average, likely to mate with men of higher economic and social status than themselves."

**Page:** 1-2  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 7.3:** Synchronicity operates *within* but not *determined by* assortative mating
- Assortative mating = **causal** (similar attract)
- Synchronicity = **acausal** (meaningful coincidence)
- Example: Woman dates men of similar education (assortative), but only one feels "meant to be" (synchronicity)
- Hypergamy is **ego-level** pattern; synchronicity transcends ego

## Chapter 9: Contemporary Crisis in Mating Systems

### Quote 18: Hypergamy's Wide-Ranging Consequences
> "The type of hypergamy discussed in this paper has potentially wide-ranging economic consequences. It may be a decisive determinant of the gender-specific distributions of parenthood and economic wealth, as well as an important contributor to the gender gap in labor earnings."

**Page:** 3  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 9.2:** Structural impacts of hypergamy on society
- Affects: parenthood rates, wealth distribution, gender wage gap
- Dating apps amplify hypergamy → intensify these consequences
- Choice overload → women filter by status more efficiently → bottom 50% men invisible
- Contemporary crisis = **accelerated hypergamy** via technology

---

### Quote 19: Temporal Stability (No Decline)
> "There is no clear evidence of either increasing or decreasing hypergamy over the study period."

**Page:** 21  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 9.3:** Hypergamy is **stable** across 23 birth cohorts (1952-1975)
- Despite: massive increases in female education, labor force participation, egalitarian norms
- Conclusion: Hypergamy resistant to cultural change
- **Hypothesis:** May have intensified post-2010 with dating app adoption (not in this dataset)
- Future research: Compare pre-app (1952-1975) to post-app (2010+) cohorts

---

### Quote 20: Multiple Partnerships by Status
> "The probability of mating with multiple partners increases with earnings rank for men, whereas it decreases with earnings rank for women."

**Page:** 15 (Table 3)  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 9.4:** Serial monogamy as covert polygyny
- High-status men: **effective polygyny** via divorce and remarriage
- Low-status men: **permanent exclusion** from mating market
- High-status women: monogamous (don't "trade up" multiple times)
- Modern "monogamy" = **nominal** but not **functional** (Fayyaz, 2019)

## Chapter 10: Integration and Synthesis

### Quote 21: Parental Earnings as Predetermined Measure
> "To empirically disentangle the impacts of earnings potential on partner matching from the effects of the match on subsequent earnings, we exploit the well-established intergenerational correlation in earnings ranks; see, e.g., Dahl and DeLeire (2008), Chetty et al. (2014), ... More specifically, we rank all men and women separately on the basis of their parents' prime age labor earnings and use this rank as a strictly predetermined proxy for the offspring's own earnings-potential rank."

**Page:** 4  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 10.2:** Methodological innovation—solving simultaneity problem
- Earnings both **cause** (mate selection) and **effect** (specialization) of matching
- Parental earnings = **exogenous** variable (unaffected by marriage)
- Allows causal inference: earnings capacity → hypergamous sorting
- Model for interdisciplinary research: using economics methods to test evolutionary psychology

### Quote 23: Gender Personality Paradox
> "In addition, there is a literature arguing that gender differences in personality traits become larger in prosperous and egalitarian societies, in which women have equal opportunities as men; see, e.g., Costa et al. (2001), Schmitt et al. (2008), and Stoet and Geary (2018)."

**Page:** 22  
**Integration Notes:**
- **Section 10.4:** Gender equality paradox—more equality → greater psychological differences
- Challenges blank slate theory (social constructionism)

- Relevant to: mate preferences, personality, vocational interests
- Supports evolutionary psychology over purely sociological explanations
