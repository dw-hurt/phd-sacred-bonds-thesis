### Quote 8: Darwin's Sexual Selection Support
**Page:** 77

> "Charles Darwin proposed in the Sexual Selection Theory- which operates with intrasexual competition between members of one sex with the loser typically failing to mate and intersexual selection (also called female choice) making member of one sex discriminatory about whom they mated with- that men and women operate on different cognitive biases when it comes to human mating."

**Integration Notes:**
- Foundational sexual selection principles
- Two mechanisms: intrasexual competition + intersexual choice
- Sex differences in cognitive biases supported empirically

## CHAPTER 3: SOCIOLOGICAL/ENVIRONMENTAL DIMENSION

### Quote 9: Culture Definition
**Page:** 78

> "Culture, as defined by Jahoda (2012), are artefacts which is the material word that has been modified over history to adapt to human thought and action. It also lays the down the foundation for human development, as it comprises of successful and adaptive participation in human activities. This development aids human beings for interaction with other people; culture is additionally responsible for preserving and transmitting social norms, beliefs, conventions, etc."

**Integration Notes:**
- Culture as accumulated adaptive knowledge
- Transmits successful strategies across generations
- Foundation for Chapter 3 sociological analysis

---

### Quote 10: Culture's Central Role
**Page:** 78

> "Considering the above-mentioned definition, it can easily be implied that culture plays a vital role in our interaction with other people. Since human mating and sexual activity requires social interaction, it would be naïve to disregard the role of culture in shaping these interactions."

**Integration Notes:**
- **KEY ARGUMENT FOR CHAPTER 3**
- Mating is social, therefore cultural
- Cannot study mating without cultural context
- Challenges culture-blind evolutionary psychology

### Quote 11: Female Pressure on Male Strategies
**Page:** 78

> "The Sexual Strategies theory states that a male is likely to pursue various strategies to successfully mate with a female; as women are likely to require long-term commitment (sexual and emotional), therefore, men need to modify their mating strategies to cater to these wants. In other words, females have pressured males into adopting long-term mating strategies."

**Integration Notes:**
- Female choice drives male strategy evolution
- Long-term commitment as female preference
- Sexual selection via female preference for investing males

## BIOLOGICAL EVIDENCE FOR MONOGAMY EVOLUTION

### Quote 35: Male Biological Features
**Page:** 77

> "Modern human beings have evolved into monogamous mates; this could be attributed to the biological features of male sexual organs and reproductive behavior. Contrary to Buss's stance that men are more inclined towards pursuing short-term mating strategies, research suggests that human males are fit for monogamous mating or polygynous primates but not multi-male/female mating arrangements."

**Integration Notes:**
- **CRITICAL BIOLOGICAL EVIDENCE**
- Male anatomy indicates monogamy adaptation
- Contradicts Buss's short-term mating emphasis
- Not fit for multi-male/multi-female systems

---

### Quote 36: Sperm Competition Evidence
**Page:** 77

> "The existence of the human male Sperm Competition suggests that human males recognize his limitation, when it comes to gaining access to the reproductive abilities of a female, specifically because of his biological makeup. As mentioned by (Fletcher et al., 2015), human males have smaller testes with less sperm production, slower replacement of sperm, less vigorous sperm, lower sperm quality and lower ejaculation rates. This is in comparison to their other male counterparts, such as in Chimpanzees."

**Integration Notes:**
- **KEY BIOLOGICAL DATA**
- Male reproductive physiology indicates low sperm competition
- Smaller testes, less sperm than chimps
- Biological constraints on promiscuity
- Evidence for evolved monogamy

---

### Quote 37: Monogamy Reduces Sperm Competition
**Page:** 77

> "Monogamy reduces the problem of Sperm Competition, thus resulting in a shift towards long-term mating strategy."

**Integration Notes:**
- Evolutionary pressure toward monogamy
- Reduces male-male competition
- Shift from short-term to long-term strategy
- Biological and strategic convergence

## ANTHROPOLOGICAL EVIDENCE FOR MONOGAMY

### Quote 41: Australopithecus Shift
**Page:** 77

> "Historically, Australopithecus afarensis were polygynous- suggesting that one male would indulge in reproductive activities with more than one female. One reason for this could be that they had a shorter period of growing and reached adulthood faster than modern humans. This resulted in less parental investment which meant reproductive constraints, for both males and females, were relatively minimum. However, Australopithecus afarensis gradually shifted their mating behavior from polygamy to monogamy during the Homo Erectus era."

**Integration Notes:**
- Evolutionary trajectory: polygyny → monogamy
- Linked to increased parental investment
- Longer development period requires pair-bonding
- Phylogenetic evidence

---

### Quote 42: Eusocial Evidence
**Page:** 76-77

> "Monogamy can also be witnessed in social insects, in terms of Eusocial behavior, as mating with a single male is ancestral for all the eight independent eusocial lineages. Thus, producing concrete evidence that monogamy was critical in the evolution of eusociality. This also supports the prediction of inclusive fitness theory."

**Integration Notes:**
- Convergent evolution evidence
- Monogamy prerequisite for eusociality
- Inclusive fitness theory support
- Cross-species pattern
